> This is a really bad way to evaluate the effectiveness of multiple 
> CPUs in real world use, because the single tasks being measured have a 
> bottleneck through one processor.  In other words, It's only remotely 
> valid if are only ever trying to do one thing on your computer at once 
> - in other situations the OS will distribute different processes 
> across the CPUs.  You'll see some improvement on the benchmarks 
> because some tasks will run on the second CPU, but you won't see 
> anything close to the full utilization you're likely to experience in 
> daily use with multiple tasks running
>
> Beware using benchmarks without fully understanding the architecture.
